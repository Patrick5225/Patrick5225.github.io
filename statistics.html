Statistical Dunkirk

Statistical methods are constantly being misused with techniques such as data fishing (p-hacking). Surveys of results that report as "statistically significant" have suggested that many p values are wrong. As a result, the need for qualified statisticians have been constantly increasing in the natural sciences.

Brief overview

There is a growing problem in the field of statistics, one being the misuse of p-values. This is done through p-hacking, which can be thought of as manipulating data to make it where the desired outcome seems to be “statistically significant” enough to draw conclusions about certain ideas. Because researchers use p-hacking and tries to make insignificant results seem supposedly significant, research has become ineffectively conducted; hence, results become biased in favor of the researchers. This method of “cheating” statistics seems to provoke public skepticism towards data that claim to be statistically significant. Due to the increase of supposedly claimed “statistically significant” data being published in scientific publications, this has come to a lack of trust of statisticians and their researches that they have conducted.

In statistical researches, researchers express the confidence of their data in terms of a p-value (typically chosen to be 0.05 or 0.01), which is the probability that data is the result of random variation. If the p-value is less than the prespecified value, conductors of the research can claim evidence against their called null hypothesis, thus favoring evidence of the alternative hypothesis. However, it has become common for researchers to misuse the concept of p-values and jump straight to the positive false conclusion of their data. Several reasons may arise from the misusage of p-values: (1) multiple comparisons are made where researchers may continually modify their data until a null hypothesis can be rejected, (2) very few tests are conducted to test a p-value (we would need as many statistical tests to be conducted so we can claim that each data set is not the result of random variation) (3) researchers may try to account for variables that can make their data fall below the p-value; in other words, researchers try to get certain samples to have certain characteristics that can provide evidence against null hypothesis.
